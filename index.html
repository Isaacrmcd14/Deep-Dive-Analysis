Hypothesis test

The biggest killer wasn't in the hypothesis.

Two hypotheses went in — company bandwidth/motivation, and deal source/validation. Both matter, but a third variable dominates the loss column.

H1
Partially supported

Bandwidth kills; company health doesn't.

Raw company health barely discriminates — nearly every deal in the funnel is a growing PE roll-up (avg 4.0 advancing vs 3.8 not). What separates outcomes is bandwidth & competing priorities: budget/bandwidth averages 3.7 for advancing deals vs 2.5 for stalled/lost. Pharming is the purest H1 loss: FDA limbo + layoffs collapsed capacity to buy anything.

H2
Supported — as a survival factor

PE validation keeps deals alive, not won.

Almost all deep-dive deals are decently sourced, so source alone can't separate wins. But the pattern is clear: PE-mandated deals stall and revive (ISN quiet 5 months → came back; Imperial Dade died → restarted via Bain; GLI froze for CVC's close → restarted). Conference-sourced deals (Boise, JCI, Merck) stall and simply drift.

H3
Unhypothesized — strongest signal

Internal builds & incumbents are the #1 killer.

A competing internal AI team, in-house build, or incumbent vendor is the primary or secondary loss driver in 7 of 10 non-advancing deals: Odevo's 15-engineer team, JCI's platform org, Renuity Brain, Pave America's PAVED, DTI insourcing, Merck's Google/Microsoft deals, Paschal's incumbent Netic. This is a positioning problem, not a bandwidth problem.

The rubric

Eight variables, built to test the hypothesis — not confirm it.

Two variables operationalize each starting hypothesis. The other five are alternative explanations added deliberately, so the hypothesis had to beat rival theories rather than run unopposed. Every score (1–5) must be anchored to a verbatim transcript quote; if no evidence exists, the variable is marked N/E rather than guessed.

Variable

A · Company health / growth

Is the prospect growing, flat, or struggling? Formulated from the theory that growing companies can justify pilot spend and switching costs while stagnant ones can't. Scored from stated growth, M&A activity, market position, and distress signals.

Variable

B · Budget / bandwidth

The second half of H1: stated capacity to spend ~$20k+ AND staff a 3-month pilot. Split from A because a healthy company can still be capacity-starved. Scored from explicit budget remarks, staffing commitments, and "we're stretched" language.

Variable

C · Deal source & validation

Operationalizes H2: how they heard of InstaLILY, referral strength, PE mandate, connections to existing clients. Scored from intro provenance (PE operating partner > client referral > conference > cold) and whether a sponsor actively pushes the deal.

Variable

D · Champion strength

Alternative explanation from sales literature: deals die when nobody inside pulls. Scored on seniority, internal authority, and engagement trajectory of the main contact(s) — a senior, active champion scores 5; a solo mid-level explorer scores 2–3.

Variable

E · Use-case clarity & urgency

Controls for "they liked us but had nothing concrete to buy." Scored on how specific, quantified, and painful the problem is — "20 hours per quote" scores 5; "exploratory, many ideas" scores 2–3.

Variable

F · Technical / data readiness

Controls for feasibility: systems landscape, data access, integration blockers. A single modern instance scores high; 10 ERPs, no APIs, or no master data scores low.

Variable

G · Pricing / pilot friction

Controls for commercial objections independent of ability to pay: sticker shock, clawback demands, risk-share asks, model misfit (build-and-own vs recurring). 5 = price accepted without pushback.

Variable

H · Competing priorities / vendors

Controls for crowd-out: internal builds, incumbent vendors, platform commitments, transformation overload. 5 = clear field. This control variable turned out to be the strongest discriminator in the dataset.

Coding protocol: each deal was scored by an isolated analysis pass that read the transcripts blind to outcome — scores were locked before any judgment about whether the deal advanced, so the outcome couldn't color the evidence. Scoring guide · Rubric v1

How every deal was scored.

The full rubric, variable definitions, scoring anchors, and the coding protocol — reusable for scoring any future deep-dive deal.

1 · Quote-anchored scores

Each variable scored 1–5 (1 = strong negative signal, 5 = strong positive). Every score requires one verbatim quote (≤25 words) plus the call date. No evidence → N/E, never guessed.

2 · Blind coding

All eight variables are scored from transcript evidence before forming any view on the outcome. Only after scores are locked is the deal classified advancing / stalled / lost — so the outcome can't color the evidence read.

3 · Sampled reading

≤4 unique calls → read all. >4 calls → read first 2 + most recent 2 by date; pull middle calls only to resolve N/E or ambiguity. Same-date, similar-size files checked as duplicate captures and deduped.

4 · Graded confidence

Close-lost reasons graded STATED (said explicitly on a call), IMPLIED (strong contextual signal), or INFERRED (analyst judgment). Each deal also carries an evidence-quality flag (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW) so thin transcripts aren't mistaken for weak deals.

Variable

ACompany health / growth

Definition: The prospect's overall business trajectory — growing, flat, or struggling — independent of this deal.

Evidence: Stated revenue/growth remarks, M&A activity, new facilities or products, market position, distress signals (layoffs, lost revenue, "tough quarters").

5Explicit strong growth or dominant market position
3Stable / mixed signals
1Stated decline or distress
Variable

BBudget / bandwidth

Definition: The prospect's stated capacity to fund (~$20k+) and staff a 3-month pilot. This is about whether they can — money in the budget, people who can be assigned, an approver who can sign.

Evidence: Direct budget remarks ("we don't have big budgets," "I'm okay with the number"), staffing commitments ("we'll carve out two, three of our better estimators"), lean-organization language, unresolved signing authority.

5Budget affirmed and people named
3Money exists but not committed, or approver unclear
1Explicit inability to justify the cost or staff the work
Variable

CDeal source & external validation

Definition: How the deal originated and how much third-party credibility backs it.

Evidence: Intro provenance ranked roughly: PE operating-partner mandate > referral from a live client deployment > personal relationship > conference contact > cold. Plus whether a sponsor actively pushes (board pressure, portfolio program) and whether they cite proof from existing InstaLILY clients.

5PE mandate or client referral with active sponsor push
3Warm but passive connection
1Cold / no validation
Variable

DChampion strength

Definition: Whether someone inside the account has the seniority, authority, and sustained engagement to pull the deal through.

Evidence: Title/authority of the main contact, whether they recruit colleagues and executives, engagement trajectory (deepening vs. going quiet, handoffs to junior people, no-shows).

5Senior owner actively driving ("we'll throw whatever we can at this")
3Engaged but mid-level or needs upward approval
1Evaluator-not-puller, or champion churned away
Variable

EUse-case clarity & pain urgency

Definition: How specific, quantified, and painful the problem is.

Evidence: Quantified pain ("20 hours per quote," "22% close ratio, goal is 30") vs. exploratory idea lists ("foreman in a box, scheduler in a box"); whether the use case stays stable or churns across calls.

5Single quantified, urgent problem
3Real pain but diffuse or shifting scope
1Purely exploratory — "no problems today"
Variable

FTechnical / data readiness

Definition: Feasibility of actually deploying — systems landscape, data access, integration blockers.

Evidence: Single modern instance vs. many fragmented ERPs/CRMs, API availability, master-data maturity, compliance constraints on data or LLM use.

5Unified modern stack with APIs
3Fragmented but workable
1No APIs, no master data, or hard compliance blockers
Variable · 5 = no friction

GPricing / pilot-structure friction

Definition: Commercial objections independent of ability to pay — the reaction to the price and deal structure itself.

Evidence: Sticker shock, clawback / risk-share / holdback demands, ROI-math challenges, commercial-model misfit (wants build-and-own vs. recurring), contract redline battles.

5Price accepted without pushback
3Friction raised but resolved cooperatively
1Explicit rejection of price or model
Variable · 5 = clear field

HCompeting priorities / vendors

Definition: External and internal alternatives crowding out the deal — what else is competing for the same problem, budget, and attention.

Evidence: Internal AI/engineering builds on the same use case, incumbent vendors, enterprise platform commitments (Google, Microsoft), parallel transformations (ERP migrations, M&A integration), multi-vendor RFPs.

5No alternatives in play
3Other initiatives exist but not directly overlapping
1Direct internal build or incumbent actively competing for the same scope

B vs. H — the key distinction

B asks: can they do this deal? Capacity — money, people, approval. H asks: will this deal win against everything else they could do instead? Competition for the slot.

High B, low H — displaced

Odevo said "we can spend the money" (B fine) but the CTO's 15-engineer internal team was shipping the same product (H = 1, fatal). Low-H deals get actively displaced: Paschal → incumbent Netic, DTI → insourced.

Low B, high H — starved

Pharming had no competing vendor, but FDA limbo and layoffs left no budget or staff to buy anything. Low-B deals stall softly: unreturned emails, "drinking from a fire hose."

Both discriminate advancing from stuck (B gap ≈ 1.2, H gap ≈ 1.1 on group averages), but H shows up as the primary stated loss reason most often — 7 of 10 non-advancing deals.

Methodology

Blind-coded, evidence-first, then compared.

  1. Fixed rubric, blind coding

    Each deal coded in isolation against 8 variables (company health, bandwidth, deal source, champion, use-case urgency, data readiness, pricing friction, competing priorities) — scored from transcript evidence before any outcome judgment, each score anchored to a verbatim quote.

  2. Outcome signal after scoring

    Only after scoring was each deal classified advancing / stalled / lost, with the close-lost reason graded STATED, IMPLIED, or INFERRED. Long deals were sampled (first 2 + last 2 calls) with middle calls pulled only to resolve gaps.

  3. Cross-deal comparison

    Score averages compared across outcome groups to find discriminating variables, with evidence-quality flags so thin transcripts (e.g. BMW's no-notetaker policy) aren't mistaken for weak deals.

Caveats: Outcome labels are transcript-inferred and partially confirmed — OMB Valves was manually reclassified to stalled (blind read said advancing) and remains unconfirmed. "Advancing" ≠ closed-won: several deals end at unsigned SOWs. Evidence quality varies by deal; BMW and Pave America have systematically thin records. n=17 — treat gaps as directional, not statistical proof.
